About

The energy-efficiency VIP supports Georgia Tech's SMART Campus energy priorities by using campus spaces as a living lab. Students explore energy efficiency strategies, evaluate opportunities in the Institute's most energy-intensive buildings, and develop cost-saving plans for clean energy improvements.

Goal

Install solar panels at Clough to support renewable energy use and create a local energy source that can operate independently from Georgia Tech's main power grid.

Solar Panel Info

Figure illustrates the impact of system losses on annual energy production, showing a range from ideal conditions (657 kWh/year) to extreme loss scenarios (291 kWh/year), with the expected typical output of approximately 568 kWh/year.

The system uses a Sunbolt Adventurer 400, a high-efficiency crystalline silicon photovoltaic (PV) solar panel that converts sunlight directly into electricity through the photovoltaic effect. The panel consists of semiconductor silicon cells that absorb incoming solar radiation; when photons strike the material, they excite electrons and generate a flow of direct current (DC) electricity. This system has a rated power output of approximately 400 W (0.4 kW) and is expected to produce about 584 kWh of energy annually under typical conditions, making it well-suited for small-scale energy demands. The generated electricity is stored in an integrated 100Ah (approximately 1.2 kWh) battery system, which allows excess energy produced during peak sunlight hours to be retained for later use, ensuring a consistent power supply even when sunlight is unavailable. The battery is capable of recharging from 50% capacity in roughly three hours, enabling it to meet fluctuating usage demands efficiently. Together, these components form a self-contained energy system that captures solar energy, converts it into usable electrical power, stores it, and delivers it on demand to devices such as phones and laptops, providing a reliable and renewable source of electricity independent of the grid.

Battery Storage

The generated electricity is stored in a battery so it can be used later when sunlight is unavailable, such as during cloudy periods or after peak sun hours. The battery can recharge from 50% capacity in about 3 hours, allowing it to keep up with typical usage demands. The stored energy is then supplied to devices like phones or laptops in a controlled manner, ensuring consistent output regardless of fluctuations in sunlight.

How the system works

Sunlight hits the solar panel
Solar radiation is absorbed by the photovoltaic cells.
Electricity is generated (DC power)
Excited electrons create an electric current through the silicon cells.
Energy is stored in the battery
Excess energy produced during peak sunlight hours is stored for later use.
Power is delivered to users
The stored energy is used to charge devices or power small loads when needed.

Cost

The proposed solar panel system has an estimated upfront cost of approximately $1,460, which includes the 400W panel, battery storage, and mounting materials. Under typical Atlanta conditions, the system is expected to generate about 584 kWh annually, resulting in roughly $70 per year in avoided electricity costs based on a campus rate of $0.12 per kWh. Over a 15-year period, this amounts to approximately $1,050 in total savings, which is insufficient to fully recover the initial investment within that timeframe. As a result, the system has an estimated payback period of around 21 years, exceeding the 15-year analysis window. Maintenance costs are relatively low, estimated at $10-$30 per year, though neglecting upkeep could reduce energy output by 10-15% due to factors such as dust accumulation. Additionally, about 75% of the system's energy production occurs during peak-demand periods, offsetting approximately 438 kWh annually and providing an estimated $66 in peak electricity value. While the system does not offer strong financial returns under current electricity rates, it still provides meaningful benefits in terms of peak demand reduction, grid resilience, and long-term sustainability goals.
